Key Exchange

The Problem:

How can two parties agree to use the same secret key to encrypt information if one party never explicitly tells the other which key they are using.

IT'S IMPOSSIBLE

Despite rudimentary cryptography being in use since before the Roman Empire it wasn't until 1976 that we realized secret key exchange over a public channel was possible.

Diffie & Hellman, Right?

While the United States researchers Whitfield Diffie and Martin Helman have been the folks long credited with soliving this problem, in 1997 the British Spy Agency GCHQ declassified the research of James Ellis, Clifford Cocks and Malcolm Williamson proving they had solved the problem in 1975.

Fortunately the Real Math Works

  • Start by publically agreeing on a prime number and primitive root, for this example 2 is a primitive root of 13
    • Easy to go in one direction 234 mod 13 = X
    • Hard to go the other way 10 = 2X mod 13
  • Each side has their own secret number Y1 and Y2, which they use to compute a number they can share
    • 2y mod 13

  • That shared number is then raised to the power of each sides secret number and each side ends up with the same final number!
